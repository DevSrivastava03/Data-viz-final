Would Faith Hill
Still Be a Hit?

In 2000, Faith Hill's "Breathe" finished the year as the top song on the Billboard Hot 100. Since then, streaming platforms, playlists, and TikTok have pulled the sound of a hit in new directions. This project looks at twenty four years of Billboard entries paired with Spotify audio features to ask a simple question: if "Breathe" came out today, would it still climb to number one?

Chapter 1 / Big picture

Three decades of hit music in one view

Before comparing one song to the present day, it helps to understand how the sound of a typical hit has shifted over time. The chart on the right compresses the 2000s, 2010s, and 2020s into three numbers: average tempo for each decade.

In the early two thousands, charting tracks leaned into higher energy radio pop. The following decade saw a slight cooling as EDM peaks mixed with slower hip hop and ballads. By the 2020s, hits settle into a slightly slower and more controlled range that works well for playlists and background listening.

"Breathe" was written for that first decade climate. Its tempo sits above the average even for the 2000s.

Chapter 2 / Tempo

The pulse of the charts slows down

Tempo is one of the clearest differences between "Breathe" and many present day hits. The line chart tracks the average beats per minute for songs in this dataset from 2000 to 2024. The golden dot marks the tempo of "Breathe" in that landscape.

The trend is not a straight line downward. There are bumps around the mid two thousands and early two thousand tens when dance pop and EDM pulled averages higher. But the general direction is toward slightly slower songs, which fit well into long playlists and looping streams.

Chapter 3 / Length

Hit songs keep getting shorter

Early two thousands singles often ran longer than four minutes, a format that worked for radio programming and slow build ballads. Streaming platforms reward repeat plays instead. Shorter tracks can deliver more streams in the same listening window.

The chart shows the average track length in minutes. The marker for "Breathe" shows that its four minute running time is now an outlier.

Long for today’s single driven landscape.

Chapter 4 / Loudness

The long fight for loudness plateaus

For years mastering engineers pushed tracks louder to stand out on radio and CD. This practice, often called the loudness war, filled waves with dense and compressed mixes. Streaming platforms later added volume normalization, which levels playback, but the aesthetic of high impact, high density sound remains.

"Breathe" was not mastered as aggressively as many recent hits. In a normalized streaming context it would still be audible, but its softer profile might feel less immediate next to modern pop and hip hop.

Chapter 5 / Energy and movement

Intensity stays high while movement shifts

Spotify's energy and danceability measures describe how intense a track feels and how easy it is to move to it. This dual line chart follows both values across time. The two golden points show where "Breathe" lands on each scale.

Energy levels have stayed fairly strong across the period, but danceability rises in the 2010s as rhythmic hip hop and electronic pop dominate the charts. "Breathe" has respectable energy yet more modest danceability compared to many recent songs designed for clubs, challenges, and short form video.

Chapter 6 / Mood drift

Popular music grows less bright on average

Several studies argue that popular music sounds less positive today than it did at the start of the century. Spotify's valence feature offers a way to see that shift numerically. Higher values indicate brighter, more cheerful sounding songs.

The line on the right leans downward through the late two thousands and early two thousand tens, when emo influenced hip hop, darker electronic textures, and introspective pop move to the center of the charts. "Breathe" sits on the upper side of that scale, which would make it stand out as unusually optimistic in many modern playlists.

Chapter 7 / Genres

Every style carries its own audio fingerprint

Grouping the dataset into broad genres reveals familiar patterns. Hip hop leans high in danceability. Rock carries stronger average energy. Country and pop sit nearer the center, often with brighter moods and more acoustic instruments.

Faith Hill's song blends country and pop traits. It fits the energy and valence profile of country hits from the early 2000s, but the overall charts have shifted toward hip hop and hybrid pop that sound quite different from this palette.

Chapter 8 / Verdict

Would "Breathe" still be a hit?

The final two charts bring everything together. The first compares the musical structure of "Breathe" against the typical 2020s hit. The second compares their emotional profile: energy, danceability, mood, and acousticness.

On nearly every dimension, "Breathe" sits to the side of the cluster. It is faster, longer, quieter, and more acoustic. It is also brighter and slightly less dance focused than many current number ones. That does not mean the song is weak. It means that the market around it has moved.

The original version would likely feel nostalgic rather than contemporary if it appeared on the charts today. A remix that trims the length, boosts loudness, and leans into modern drum and bass choices might have a better shot at repeating its earlier success.
